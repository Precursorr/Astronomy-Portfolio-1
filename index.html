Opportunity Rover!

On July 8, 2003, the Opportunity Rover launched from Earth and 7 months later, landed on Mars. 'Oppy', as she was nicknamed, was only supposed to last 90 days on mission. Instead, she explored Mars for 15 years and traveled 45 km—the longest Mars mission ever—from one that was supposed to be the shortest. Unfortunately, on February 13, 2019, she suffered critical malfunctions. The NASA mission control overseeing Oppy tried everything they could to fix her, but to no avail. The team sent one last transmission, the song 'I'll be seeing you', as a goodbye. Oppy's last message? 'My battery is low, and it's getting dark.'

The Universe!

The universe is estimated to be almost 14 billion years old, with around 90 billion light years visible to us—two numbers truly incomprehensible to us. If you were to study all the information about the universe, a lifetime would not be enough to cover everything. Instead, here are some general facts that might just blow your mind.

  • There are more stars in the universe than there are grains of sand on Earth.
  • At the heart of every galaxy is a 'Supermassive Black Hole' The Milky Way Black Hole has an estimated 4.3 million solar masses—that is, 4.3 million times as massive as our sun. The largest known black hole, Ton 618, has an estimated 66 billion solar masses.
  • If we wanted the Earth to become a black hole, we would need to squash everything down to the size of a pea nut.
  • The hardest theoretical material is nuclear pasta, which can only be found in the second most violent celestial object, the core of a neutron star. Here, gravity is so strong that electrons are ripped away from atoms, and you end up with proton and neutron 'pasta'.
  • When a star dies, it has the potential to go supernova. The final element conversion is silicone to iron, because iron cannot be broken into another element and therefore cannot be used as fuel. Eventually, the fuel gets so low that the radiation pushing the star outward cannot overcome the gravity pushing inward. The core collapses in on itself at 25% the speed of light. Either a neutron star will be born or a black hole will form. This is how nebulae form too!
  • Nebulas are giant gas clouds of beauty spanning many light years across.
  • Most stars we see in the right sky are already dead. The reason we see them is because space is so vast that even the speed of light seems slow. The death event of that star is yet to reach our eyes. When we look up at night, we are in fact looking into the past of the universe.
