The Solar System


Our Sun

The sun is a G-type star, or yellow dwarf, as they're more commonly known. It was formed from the collection of a huge nitrogen gas cloud that collasped in on itself, eventually triggering fusion and lighting up.

The sun makes up 99.8% of all mass in the solar system, has an inner core temperature of 15 million degrees Celsius, and is almost 1.4 billion km wide.


Earth

Earth is the third planet in the solar system, is 150 million km away from our sun, and is considered to be in the Goldilocks zone, the position a planet can be in around a star that allows life to thrive.

Our little blue rock is over 4.5 billion years old and has sustained life for more than half that time. We are merely specks on this impossible-to-imagine timescale.


Moon

The Moon is Earth's celestial satellite that orbits our planet every 28 days and is tidal-locked, meaning the same side always faces the planet. This is due to it's axis rotation and orbit around the Earth matching.

It's said that the moon formed when two Mars-sized planets smashed into each other. The end result was Earth and its moon.


Mercury

Mercury is the first planet in the solar system and is 58 million km away from the sun. Being so close, it's impossible for life to form here, as temperatures range from 430 degrees Celsius during the day to minus 180 degrees Celsius during the night.

A year on Mercury is only 88 Earth days with it's short orbit around the sun.


Venus

Venus is the second planet in the solar system, and much like Mercury, it is too close to the sun to sustain life, but despite being 108 million km away from the sun, Venus is actually hotter.

A day on Venus lasts longer than a year, spins clockwise on its axis, and has 90 times the atmospheric pressure of Earth.


Mars

Mars is the fourth planet in our solar system and is considered to be a planet that once harbored life and could possibly do so again with terraforming efforts.

With plans to colonize Mars one day, we could soon call it humanities second home.

Mars is named after the Roman God of War and is the second-smallest planet after Mercury.


Jupiter

Jupiper is the fifth planet in the solar system and is the largest of the four gas giants, as well as the rest of the planets. Jupiter is considered a failed star as its composition is that of our sun, which is mostly hydrogen.

Jupiter has twice the mass of the rest of the planets combined, has a circumference of 439,264 km, and has 312 times the surface area of Earth (61.5 billion square km).


Saturn

Saturn is the sixth planet in the solar system and is definitely the most unique with its orbiting ring. Saturn's rings are thought to have evolved from the debris of two icy moons that collided and shattered a few hundred million years ago.

Saturn is the king of the moons, having 146. It is a gas giant and takes 29 years to orbit the sun.


Uranus

Uranus is the seventh planet in the solar system, also being a gas giant. Uranus has a unique color due to its methane gas atmosphere.

Uranus is the coldest plant in the solar system, even though it isn't the furthest away. It orbits the sun on its side and has rings that aren't visible to traditional telescopes.


Neptune

Neptune is the eighth and is considered the final planet in the solar system, and it too has rings that aren't visible.

Compared to Earth, a year on Neptune lasts 165 years, is 30 times further away, and is twice as dense as the other gas giants. It has only completed one orbit around the sun since it was first discovered.
